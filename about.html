The comeback

Six months ago I couldn't run to the end of my street.

In a few weeks I'll be toeing the start line at Surf Coast Century as a brand ambassador. I'm a physio — I know how bodies work, how they break, and how they come back. And still, injury sidelined me for longer than I'd like to admit.

Ultra Normal is what I'm calling this whole thing. Because that's what it feels like — ordinary person, doing something that feels extraordinary, one normal day at a time. No elite training squad. No sponsor cheques. Just consistent, intelligent effort and a willingness to be honest about how it's going.

I've handed my training plan to an AI coach — which is a strange thing for a physio to do, and I'll admit that upfront. But it's also given me something to talk about honestly: what an AI gets right about training, where I override it with my clinical brain, and what the intersection of technology and body awareness actually looks like when you're rebuilding from injury.

The physio side

I work with runners, return-to-sport athletes, and people who just want to move without hurting. My particular interest is load management — helping people understand how much is enough, and how to push without breaking.

If you're training for something and want someone who understands both the performance side and the injury prevention side, that's the work I do.

What Ultra Normal stands for

The name is intentional. "Ultra" because that's the world I'm training in — long distances, big efforts, trail running culture. "Normal" because I think the most interesting stories in endurance sport aren't the elite ones. They're the ordinary people doing extraordinary things in the margins of their lives.

You don't have to be fast. You don't have to be lean. You just have to keep going.
